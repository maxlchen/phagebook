What are Phages?

Bacteriophages or phages are viruses that infect bacteria and have the ability to destroy their host cell. A Phage infects it’s their host by attaching itself to the surface and inserts its DNA into the bacterium. The phage DNA then replicates itself to assemble new phages or integrates into the bacterial chromosome. Upon assembly of phages, the new progeny burst through the bacterium thus successfully killing the bacteria. In the absence of assembly, the bacterium will carry the new genes from the phage.



(A. Campbell, 2003)

Why Phage Therapy?

Why should the United States allocate resources to phage therapy? Why are bacteriophages a reasonable alternative to antibiotic treatments? What the Phage? The use of Bacteriophages as a treatment to bacterial infection was not as widely adopted in the U.S. after its initial discovery in the 1920s due to a lack of knowledge on phages and the ease of antibiotic use at the time (Kutter, et al). That being said, due to the rise in antibiotic resistance in many common infectious bacteria, the efficacy of those antibiotics we have relied on for so long is dwindling. We need somewhere to turn, and phage therapy is the answer. In cost, an all-important factor, phage also shines. The cost of phage production (host growth and phage purification) is comparable with pharmaceutical production, but the cost of isolation and characterization of phages is relatively low, compared with competing treatments (Loc-Carillo and Abedon). With a more effective treatment at lower cost, phage therapy represents a significant victory for lowering the strain of health care costs for U.S. citizens, especially considering the prevalence of bacterial infection patients, which can be as much as 722,000 cases annually (CDC Healthcare-associated infections). Education on, investment in, and awareness of bacteriophage therapy is the future of bacterial infection treatment .
